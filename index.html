Sal Swims Against the Tide
By Your One and Only Fairy Greenmother
Sal is a salmon who lives in a large river, and today is a special day for Sal.

It's that time of year for him to swim upstream and go back home.
However, Sal thinks that something is off. The water feels too warm, and it's uncomfortable and perhaps even oppressive for salmon like him.

Sal's fishy friends, however, don't think anything is wrong.
None of the fish notice anything -- do you?

Tap around to make some observations.
Sal says bye to his friends and starts his journey upstream. Along the way, he notices a large dam blocking his way.

Sal realizes that there are fewer creatures present now than there were before. Why is that?

Maybe the dam is blocking nutrients.

What's more, there could be cooler water and more nutrients released if the dam is broken
Sal notices some cracks in the dam--

If I swim into them enough times, can I weaken and break down the dam?

Tap on the cracks to have Sal weaken the dam.
Hooray! With the dam opening, Sal was able reach upstream and complete his goal successfully.

Water rushes out and the nutrients and cooler water over time allow life to thrive in these areas.

It's good Sal could reach home to create a new generation of fish, but many dams around the world are becoming problematic and climate change stops for no one.

Bad Sign: Salmon Louse

Salmon louse is an aquatic parasite that act like sea lice and can infest Salmon and other fishes. Increased sea lice infestations, which scientists say are caused by salmon farms, threaten the already-vulnerable wild Pacific salmon populations in western Canada, worrying conservationists and First Nations.

Bad Sign: Algal Bloom

Warmer temperatures prevent water from mixing, allowing algae to grow thicker and faster. Warmer water is easier for small organisms to move through and allows algae to float to the surface faster. Algal blooms absorb sunlight, making water even warmer and promoting more blooms.

Bad Sign: Dead Coral

When corals become stressed and begin to bleach the first sign is paling, then part bleached, and then whole bleached when more than 90% of the coral colony is affected. If coral colonies remain bleached for an extended period of time and do not regain their symbiotic zooxanthellae, they will die. Stony coral that is no longer covered in coral tissue often becomes covered in algae giving it a grey or reddish tinge. Over time other reef organisms may begin growing on the dead coral, but once the coral tissue has died it will not return.
